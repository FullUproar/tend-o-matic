# Indiana Prohibition-State Findings for Tend-O-Matic

## Bottom line

Indiana remains a prohibition state for marijuana. An Indiana-based SaaS company serving licensed dispensaries in Michigan and Illinois should avoid any conduct that could be characterized as possessing, delivering, financing delivery, storing, transporting, brokering, or taking title to cannabis or cannabis proceeds in Indiana. The safer posture is to operate as a neutral B2B software and compliance-data provider, with no physical cannabis handling, no in-state cannabis transactions, no inventory custody, no payment flow custody unless counsel clears the model, and no Indiana-facing cannabis retailer activity.

This is not legal advice; the practical recommendation is to obtain cannabis counsel covering Indiana, Michigan, Illinois, federal CSA/FinCEN/payment-risk, and SaaS terms/insurance before onboarding stores.

## Current Indiana legal signals reviewed

The Indiana State Department of Agriculture's hemp page states that the Office of the Indiana State Chemist is responsible for licensing agricultural hemp growers, handlers, and seed producers. It notes that the 2018 Farm Bill legalized industrial hemp and provided a federal framework, and that USDA approved the Indiana State Hemp Plan for commercially growing and processing hemp. This confirms Indiana has a regulated hemp lane, but not a regulated adult-use marijuana retail lane.

A secondary publication of 2025 Indiana Code section 35-48-4-11 states that knowingly or intentionally possessing marijuana, hash oil, hashish, or salvia; growing/cultivating marijuana; or failing to destroy marijuana plants known to be growing on one's premises is possession of marijuana, hash oil, hashish, or salvia, generally a Class B misdemeanor, with elevated classifications in certain situations.

A secondary publication of 2025 Indiana Code section 35-48-4-10 states that knowingly or intentionally manufacturing, financing manufacture, delivering, or financing delivery of marijuana/hash oil/hashish/salvia, or possessing with intent to do so, is dealing in marijuana, hash oil, hashish, or salvia, generally a Class A misdemeanor with elevated felony categories based on amount, prior offenses, sale to a minor, or deceptive low-THC hemp extract packaging.

Because the official Indiana General Assembly code site is dynamic and difficult to extract, these statutory excerpts should be verified against official state sources or counsel before relying on exact text.

## Implications for an Indiana-headquartered cannabis POS SaaS

| Risk area | Practical implication | Recommended posture |
|---|---|---|
| Physical possession | Indiana criminal law treats possession/growing of marijuana as illegal. | Do not store, transport, sample, receive, test, or demonstrate real cannabis in Indiana. |
| Delivery/financing delivery language | Dealing statute includes delivery and financing delivery. | Avoid taking title, arranging product transfers, brokering wholesale movement, or financing cannabis inventory. |
| Payments | Cannabis payments remain federally sensitive and Indiana is not a licensed cannabis state. | Treat payment processing as a separate counsel-reviewed module; consider integrations where the licensed operator and payment provider carry cannabis-specific obligations. |
| Customer support | Support staff in Indiana may view transaction/inventory data for out-of-state regulated stores. | Position support as software support, not transaction authorization or product advice; log access and implement confidentiality controls. |
| Data hosting | Cannabis inventory and sales data may be hosted or accessed from Indiana. | Use strong contractual language, security controls, audit logs, and data-processing posture; counsel should review whether any state-specific exposure exists. |
| Marketing | Indiana residents cannot buy adult-use cannabis in-state. | Avoid marketing to Indiana consumers or implying Indiana dispensary availability; market B2B to licensed operators in legal states. |
| Entity structure | An Indiana LLC/corporation can sell software, but cannabis-adjacent revenue may affect banking/insurance. | Consider counsel/accountant review of entity location, possible Delaware entity or state-specific subsidiaries, banking, insurance, and contracts. |
| Employee policies | Indiana employees may be in a prohibition environment. | Adopt no-cannabis-handling policies and compliance training for staff. |

## Product implications

Tend-O-Matic should be designed so the system helps licensed stores comply without becoming the cannabis operator. The software should record and validate transactions, but legal responsibility for cannabis sales should remain with the licensed dispensary. Terms of service should make clear that stores are responsible for licensure, customer eligibility, inventory accuracy, taxes, and legal compliance; Tend-O-Matic provides software tooling and compliance alerts, not legal advice or cannabis operating authority.

## Immediate counsel questions

1. Can an Indiana entity safely provide POS/compliance software to licensed dispensaries in Michigan and Illinois without being viewed as facilitating or financing cannabis distribution under Indiana law?
2. Should the operating entity be formed outside Indiana, with Indiana as an office/engineering location rather than the contracting counterparty?
3. What language should be included in SaaS agreements to avoid possession, custody, title, agency, brokerage, delivery, payment custody, or cannabis-operating characterization?
4. Are there banking, insurance, tax, or employment restrictions triggered by cannabis SaaS revenue while headquartered in Indiana?
5. How should Tend-O-Matic structure payment-processing integrations, if any, so it does not touch funds in a way that increases federal/state risk?
